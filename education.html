Biblical and Historical Foundations

Zakat is not merely a tax; it is one of the Five Pillars of Islam. Derived from the root word 'Zaka', which means to grow, to purify, and to be blessed. When a Muslim gives Zakat, they are not giving "charity" in the Western sense, but rather returning a portion of wealth that rightfully belongs to the poor as ordained by Allah.

Spiritual Purification

Zakat cleanses the heart from the disease of greed (Bukhl) and teaches the believer that wealth is a trust (Amanah) from the Creator.

Economic Circulation

By taxing stagnant wealth, Islam encourages investment and discourages hoarding, ensuring money flows through the economy.

Understanding Nisab Thresholds

Nisab is the minimum amount of wealth a Muslim must possess before they are obligated to pay Zakat. This system ensures that only those with surplus wealth contribute.

  • Gold Standard: 87.48 grams (7.5 Tola) - Historically used for those who hold wealth in gold.
  • Silver Standard: 612.36 grams (52.5 Tola) - The most common threshold used for cash and business assets today.
  • Threshold Choice: Most modern scholars recommend using the Silver Nisab to maximize the benefit for the poor.

The 2.5% Math & Beyond

While the standard rate is 2.5% (1/40th) for cash and trade, Islamic law provides a sophisticated variable rate system for different types of income:

Commercial Assets

Rate: 2.5%. Includes Cash, Gold, Silver, Stocks, and Business Inventory.

Agricultural (Ushr)

Rate: 5% or 10%. 10% if watered by rain; 5% if artificial irrigation is used.

Minerals (Rikaz)

Rate: 20%. Applies to hidden treasures or natural resources discovered on private land.

Where does the Money go?

Surah At-Tawbah (Verse 60) defines exactly who is eligible to receive Zakat. It cannot be used for building mosques or public works; it must be given to people:

1. Al-Fuqara

The Poor (Those who have some income but not enough).

2. Al-Masakin

The Needy (Those with absolutely no means of survival).

3. Al-Amilina

Zakat Administrators (The staff who collect and distribute).

4. Al-Mu'allafah

Reconciling Hearts (New Muslims or those close to Islam).

5. Ar-Riqaab

Emancipation (Historically for slaves; now for those trapped in debt bondage).

6. Al-Gharimin

The Debtors (People overwhelmed by essential debts).

7. Fi Sabilillah

In the path of Allah (Educational and religious struggle).

8. Ibnus-Sabil

The Wayfarer (Travelers stranded without funds).
